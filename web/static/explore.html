Question Review

Two Kinds of Uncertainty

When we say something has a "70% chance", we might mean two very different things. Understanding the distinction is fundamental to why confidence calibration matters — and why humans are often poorly calibrated.

Aleatory Uncertainty: The Dice

Roll a fair six-sided die. The probability of rolling a 1 is exactly 1/6. This is aleatory uncertainty — irreducible randomness inherent in the system. No amount of extra information changes it. The die has no memory, no hidden state. The probability is a property of the physical process itself.

A coin flip is 50/50. A well-shuffled deck gives each card a 1/52 chance. These are frequencies — run the experiment a million times and the ratios converge. You can bet rationally on these because the probability is the long-run frequency.

Epistemic Uncertainty: The Weather

An "80% chance of rain" is fundamentally different. Tomorrow either will or will not rain — it is not a repeatable random experiment. The 80% reflects our state of knowledge, not a physical frequency. It is epistemic uncertainty — uncertainty that could, in principle, be reduced with more information (better sensors, finer models, more compute).

When a student says "I'm 90% sure the answer is B", they are expressing epistemic confidence. But humans are notoriously bad at this. Studies consistently show that when people say "90% sure", they are correct only about 70–75% of the time. Our subjective certainty outruns our actual reliability.

Why This Matters for Betting

The HLCC scoring system is designed around the idea that confidence is a bet. If you are correct, you win 1 + c. If wrong, you lose 2c². The optimal bet depends on your true probability of success:

c* = p / 4(1 − p)

At p = 50%, the rational bet is only c = 0.25. Even at p = 80%, the optimal bet is c = 1.0. The curve is asymmetric by design: the quadratic penalty means overconfidence is punished much more harshly than underconfidence.

Why massive bets are irrational
If you could truly "know" you had a 95% chance, the naive formula gives c* = 4.75 — betting nearly 5× your stake. But nobody truly knows their probability with that precision. The 95% itself carries uncertainty: maybe the question is ambiguous, maybe your reasoning has a flaw you haven't noticed, maybe the answer key is wrong. Each of these is an additional source of epistemic uncertainty that caps the rational bet well below the naive formula.

This is why we clamp c to [0, 1] in the standard system and why humans instinctively refuse to "bet the house" even when they feel very confident. That instinct is not irrational — it is an implicit recognition that their 95% might actually be 80%, and the cost of being wrong at c = 4.75 is catastrophic.

Confidence vs Probability

The three-level confidence system (1, 2, 3) maps to c = 0, 0.5, 1.0. It deliberately discretises the bet, forcing a coarse but honest self-assessment. This works because:

  • Humans are bad at fine-grained probability estimation but good at ordinal ranking ("more sure" vs "less sure")
  • Three levels maps cleanly to "might be wrong", "probably right", and "know this"
  • The asymmetric payoff structure means level 3 is only rational when accuracy exceeds 80%

The unbounded mode lets you enter a percentage and see the mathematically rational bet. This exposes the gap between how people feel about their confidence and what a rational agent would actually stake. Most people discover they should be betting much less aggressively than their gut feeling suggests.
